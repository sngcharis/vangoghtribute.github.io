About Vincent van Gogh

Early Life and Education

Vincent Willem van Gogh was born on 30 March 1853 into an upper-middle-class family in Groot-Zundert, Netherlands.

Van Gogh was a serious and thoughtful child. He was taught at home by his mother and a governess, and in 1860 was sent to the village school.

In 1864, he was placed in a boarding school where he felt abandoned, and campaigned to come home. In 1866, his parents sent him to the middle school in Tilburg, where he was deeply unhappy.

In March 1868, he abruptly returned home. He later wrote that his youth was "austere and cold, and sterile".

In July 1869, Van Gogh's uncle obtained a position for him at the art dealers. By this time, Van Gogh was fluent in French, German and English, as well as his native Dutch.

In June of 1873, Van Gogh was transferred to the Groupil Gallery in London.

Pastoral Days

In April 1876 he returned to England to take unpaid work as a supply teacher in a small boarding school. However, it did not work out and he left to become a Methodist minister's assistant.

In 1876, he returned home at Christmas for six months and took work at a bookshop in Dordrecht. He was unhappy in the position and immersed himself in religion, and became increasingly pious and monastic.

In 1877, his family sent him to live with his uncle in Amsterdam to support his religious conviction and his desire to become a pastor.

Van Gogh failed his theology entrance examination and left his uncle's house in July 1878.

In January 1879, he took up a post as a missionary at a coal-mining district of Borinage in Belgium.

To show support for his impoverished congregation, he gave up his comfortable lodgings at a bakery to a homeless person, and moved to a small hut where he slept on straw.

His squalid living conditions did not endear him to church authorities, who dismissed him for "undermining the dignity of the priesthood".

Finding Solace in Art

In the fall of 1880, Van Gogh decided to move to Brussels and become an artist. Though he had no formal art training, his younger brother, Theo offered to support van Gogh financially.

He registered at the Académie in November 1880, where he studied anatomy and the standard rules of modelling and perspective.

Van Gogh's art helped him stay emotionally balanced. In 1885, he began work on what is considered to be his first masterpiece,The Potato Eaters.

Van Gogh moved to Paris in March 1886 where he shared Theo's rue Laval apartment.

When he first saw the impressionism artwork in Paris, he was inspired by the color and light. He began studying at Fernand Cormon's studio.

Artistic Breakthrough

In February 1888, Van Gogh sought refuge in Arles.

The time in Arles became one of Van Gogh's more prolific periods. He completed 200 paintings, and more than 100 drawings and watercolors.

His style became looser and more expressive. It also excel in his use of color.

Mental Health

In December 1888, after an altercation with his friend, Van Gogh returned to his room, where he was assaulted by voices and severed part of his left ear with a razor.

Van Gogh was found unconscious the next morning by a policeman and taken to hospital.

Van Gogh had no recollection of the event, suggesting that he may have suffered an acute mental breakdown. The hospital diagnosis was "acute mania with generalized delirium"

For several months, Van Gogh continually suffered from hallucinations and delusions.

Asylum

Van Gogh voluntarily admitted himself to the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence on 8 May 1889.

During Van Gogh’s year at the asylum, he suffered from distressing mental attacks, incapacitating him for a total of several months. But for most of his time, he was mentally alert and worked intensively. Painting took up almost all the hours of the day.

During the year in asylum, Van Gogh completed roughly 142 paintings.

The most famous paintings completed in the asylum includes The Starry Night, Wheat Field with Cypresses, Irises.

Death

On 27 July 1890, aged 37, Van Gogh shot himself in the chest. However, the bullet did not kill him.

The bullet was deflected by a rib and passed through his chest without doing apparent damage to his internal organs.

He passed away two days later on 29 July in the arms of his brother, Theo.

Legacy

In just over a decade, he created about 2,100 artworks, including around 900 oil paintings, most of which date from the last two years of his life.

Today, Vincent van Gogh is considered one of the greatest artists in human history. Van Gogh's works are among the world's most expensive paintings.

Van Gogh Museum

In 1973, the Van Gogh Museum opened its doors in Amsterdam to make the works of Vincent van Gogh accessible to the public.

The museum houses more than 200 paintings, 500 drawings and 750 written documents including letters to Vincent’s brother, Theo.

It became the second most popular museum in the Netherlands, regularly receiving more than 1.5 million visitors a year.
